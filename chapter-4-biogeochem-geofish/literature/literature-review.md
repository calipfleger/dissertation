## Theme: Oxygen minimum zones and pelagic fish distributions

<!-- AUTO 2026-04-26 phase-4 -->
::: {.under-review}
The expanding Pacific oxygen minimum zone (OMZ) presents a direct constraint on the vertical and horizontal distributions of commercially and ecologically important fish species. Gutiérrez-Bravo et al. (2024) show that anoxic waters in the Eastern Tropical North Pacific OMZ compress the habitable depth range for fish larvae to a thin oxycline layer, with consequences for recruitment and cross-shelf connectivity [@gutierrez2024anoxic]. At the basin scale, Byrne et al. (2024) document that the Pacific OMZ acts as a hard biogeographic boundary for shortfin mako sharks in the eastern North Pacific, with tracking data showing abrupt avoidance of low-oxygen waters regardless of thermal preference [@byrne2024pacific]. Community-level responses are captured by Galloso et al. (2025), who reconstruct 40+ years of pelagic fish community dynamics in the northern Humboldt Current and identify regime shifts tied to combined temperature and O₂ forcing; periods of elevated ENSO-driven stratification co-occur with species composition turnover [@galloso2025humboldt]. Le Grix et al. (2023) link these dynamics to compound ocean extremes — co-occurring marine heatwaves and low-oxygen events — which drive disproportionately large reductions in projected global pelagic fish biomass in Earth system models; their framework suggests that additive or multiplicative stressor interactions, rather than individual stressors alone, dominate future biogeochemical impacts on fisheries [@legrix2023extreme]. Together these papers motivate Ch4's core questions: what is the biogeochemical signature of ENSO-driven OMZ variability, and how does it propagate through the food web to commercially fished populations?

**References:** @byrne2024pacific; @galloso2025humboldt; @gutierrez2024anoxic; @legrix2023extreme

The spatial extent and vertical shoaling of oxygen minimum zones (OMZs) in the eastern Pacific constitute a first-order physical constraint on the distribution and vertical behaviour of pelagic fish. Byrne et al. [@byrne2024pacific] use satellite telemetry to show that the Pacific OMZ creates a "soft boundary" to the southern distribution of shortfin mako sharks in the Eastern North Pacific, with the distributional limit corresponding to the apex of OMZ shoaling in the North Equatorial Current; the same study predicts that projected OMZ expansion under climate change will progressively compress suitable habitat for aerobically demanding pelagic species. At the population scale, Zhou et al. [@zhou2020spatial] reconstruct the three-dimensional habitat of bigeye tuna in the Pacific using a refined ecological niche model and show that OMZ extent modulates ENSO-driven habitat shifts: El Niño conditions expand high-quality habitat northward while contracting the western equatorial band, with the mechanism linked to fluctuations in the oxycline depth and current stratification. For fish developmental stages, Gutiérrez-Bravo et al. [@gutierrez2024anoxic] sample the Eastern Tropical North Pacific OMZ across five oxygen levels and find that the wide anoxic core (extending from Costa Rica to Baja California) is nearly devoid of fish larvae, forcing early larval stages into the thin oxygenated surface layer above the OMZ — a vertical compression that would tighten further as OMZs expand. At broader trophic scales, Le Grix et al. [@legrix2023extreme] combine a global marine fish model (DBEM) with Earth System Model output to show that compound extreme ocean events — not single driver anomalies — are a necessary condition for extremely low pelagic fish biomass over 78% of the global ocean, with low oxygen in the eastern equatorial Pacific emerging as a key co-driver alongside temperature and low primary productivity. Xing et al. [@xing2023mesoscale] further reveal that mesoscale eddies modulate tuna fishing activity in midlatitude oceans through temperature and oxygen gradients in deeper water, with anticyclonic eddies (warmer, more oxygen-rich at depth) attracting fishing effort and cyclonic eddies (colder, more hypoxic) repelling it. Together, these studies establish OMZ geometry, ENSO-driven thermocline variability, and compound biogeochemical anomalies as the mechanistic bridge between ocean physics and fish population responses relevant to Chapter 4's scope.
:::

The relationship between dissolved-oxygen availability and fish distribution is not straightforwardly negative, as early models predicted. Moriarty et al. [@moriarty2020unexpected] monitored Pacific herring (*Clupea pallasii*) and Pacific hake (*Merluccius productus*) alongside their euphausiid prey in Hood Canal, Washington, during seasonal low-dissolved-oxygen episodes and found that pelagic fish abundance — especially herring — *increased* under hypoxia rather than declining, contradicting the hypothesis that predators would retreat from low-oxygen zones; this result highlights that vertical compression of both prey and predator into the surface oxygenated layer can concentrate fish rather than exclude them, and that the ecological consequences of oxygen depletion depend on the specific predator–prey spatial overlap dynamics. Complementing this observational work, Indivero et al. [@indivero2024threshold] develop a metabolic-index framework for the U.S. Pacific Coast that combines the Arrhenius temperature-dependence of oxygen tolerance with species distribution monitoring data to infer physiological oxygen thresholds without dedicated laboratory experiments; applied to sablefish (*Anoplopoma fimbria*), the model improved distributional predictions relative to simpler temperature-only models, though it did not improve predictions for longspine thornyhead (*Sebastolobus altivelis*), suggesting that the metabolic-index approach is species-specific and requires careful validation. Together these studies caution against assuming a monotonic negative relationship between OMZ expansion and fish population declines, and motivate biogeochemically-aware distributional modelling that accounts for prey availability, species-specific oxygen thresholds, and the three-dimensional restructuring of trophic interactions.
:::

Three recent studies sharpen the mechanistic picture linking marine biogeochemistry to fish population dynamics in the Eastern Tropical Pacific — the region most directly coupled to ENSO-scale variability relevant to this chapter. Clarke et al. [@clarke2022pelagic] combine a physiological vulnerability index for large pelagic fishes with Eastern Tropical Pacific catch records from 1970 to 2016, demonstrating that ocean warming is the primary driver of changes in catch composition, while oxygen limitation introduces a significant breakpoint in the sea-surface-oxygen–vulnerability relationship in the Ecuador and Galápagos EEZs; the coupling of temperature and OMZ dynamics during El Niño events amplifies this vulnerability, although only the most extreme events produce detectable compositional shifts. Taboada et al. [@taboada2022bigeye] extend this mechanistic framework to bigeye tuna (*Thunnus obesus*) across the entire Pacific basin, showing that three-dimensional biogeochemical forecasts — integrating subsurface oxygen and thermal structure from an Earth system model — outperform surface-only predictors in hindcasting CPUE regime transitions; their analysis reveals four distinct biogeochemical regimes from 1960–2016, with regime switching most pronounced in the eastern Pacific where ENSO drives basin-wide column oxygen variability. Galloso et al. [@galloso2025humboldt] examine six decades of pelagic fish community data in the northern Humboldt Current System, confirming that dissolved oxygen and temperature together explain the long-term compositional shifts, with warmer and less oxygenated conditions driving higher species diversity — consistent with the hypothesis that OMZ expansion compresses and restructures habitat, reshaping community composition across interannual and decadal scales.
:::

### Species distribution shifts under climate change and OMZ expansion in the ETP _(added 2026-04-25)_

Climate-driven expansion of oxygen minimum zones (OMZs) is a primary mechanism compressing the vertical habitat available to pelagic fish in the Eastern Tropical Pacific (ETP). Clarke et al. (2021) modelled projected distribution shifts for 505 fish and invertebrate species in the ETP under RCP 2.6 and RCP 8.5 through mid-century, finding systematic range compression toward shallower, inshore waters as OMZs expand below the shallow thermocline [@clarke2021etp]. Importantly, under RCP 8.5 the majority of species show distributional overlap loss exceeding 50% relative to the present, with the steepest shifts for species currently occupying the OMZ boundary. This is directly relevant to Chapter 4's focus on ENSO-biogeochemical linkages: ENSO events modulate thermocline depth and OMZ intensity on interannual timescales, meaning that the long-term OMZ-expansion trend is superimposed on strong interannual variability. The Chapter 4 analysis should distinguish between the ENSO-driven interannual signal (primary driver at timescales of historical fishery data) and the secular OMZ-expansion trend (relevant for projections and for interpreting late-20th-century regime shifts documented in Galloso et al. 2025 and Clarke et al. 2022).

:::
