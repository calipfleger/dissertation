## Themes

### Volcanic forcing of ENSO _(added 2026-04-17)_

Scholar Gateway · "How do volcanic eruptions modulate ENSO variability and tropical Pacific sea surface temperatures?" · 8 passages · 5 articles · 2022-12-12–2024-05-16.

The dominant mechanistic view holds that large tropical eruptions can trigger an El Niño–like response through an "ocean dynamical thermostat": aerosol-induced surface cooling produces a smaller SST drop in the eastern Pacific than the western Pacific because eastern upwelling advects deeper, already-cool water, weakening the zonal SST gradient and trade winds via the Bjerknes feedback (Liu et al., 2022). This framing is complicated by evidence that the response is *preconditioned* on initial ocean state: Dogar et al. (2024) find that positive-ENSO–preconditioned volcanic runs produce El Niño–like anomalies up to 2 °C after eruption, while neutral and La Niña initial states yield no ENSO-like modulation, implying that "volcano → El Niño" is not a deterministic rule. Proxy reconstructions tell a consistent but muted story: Liu et al. (2024) reconstruct ENSO over the last millennium from a global δ18O network and recover only a weak El Niño response one year after major eruptions, though ENSO amplitude is significantly correlated with cumulative volcanic intensity (r ≈ 0.46 in CESM-LME). A separate coral-based reconstruction from the South China Sea (665–749 CE) attributes an abrupt 39% increase in ENSO variability at ~700 CE to combined solar and volcanic forcing, demonstrating that external forcing competes with internal climate dynamics to shape tropical Pacific variability (Jiang et al., 2023). Finally, Liu et al. (2022) isolate the land-vs-ocean partition of volcanic aerosol forcing and show that aerosol over tropical African land — via monsoon suppression and Kelvin-wave westerly anomalies — is *more* effective at triggering an El Niño than aerosol confined to ocean, a mechanistic detail that most "volcano-ENSO" bulk studies obscure.

## Gaps & open questions

- Preconditioning vs. deterministic response: the literature is actively unresolved on whether tropical eruptions *cause* El Niño or merely *amplify* ongoing warm states (Dogar et al., 2024; Liu et al., 2024). Chapter analysis should stratify eruption cases by initial ENSO phase rather than averaging across events.
- Pseudo-coral δ18O signal-to-noise under volcanic forcing has not been systematically characterized across the δ18O proxy network used in Liu et al. (2024) — a sensitivity-analysis opportunity.
- Land-vs-ocean aerosol partitioning (Liu et al., 2022) is absent from most last-millennium ensemble runs, which apply zonally uniform forcing by default.

The dependence of the post-volcanic ENSO response on ocean preconditioning is increasingly recognised as a first-order modulator of response amplitude and timing. Dogar et al. [@dogar2024nao] demonstrate using GFDL-CM2.1 that El Niño-preconditioned eruptions produce El Niño-like anomalies reaching up to +2 °C in Niño 3.4, whereas neutral and La Niña-preconditioned simulations show no Pacific warming signal beyond the aerosol-induced cooling — a finding that challenges the earlier expectation, rooted in the ocean dynamical thermostat mechanism [@clement1996ocean], that La Niña preconditioning would amplify the post-eruption El Niño response via thermocline reservoir release. Complementing this, Pausata et al. [@pausata2023revisiting] use sensitivity experiments with regionally partitioned aerosol loading to show that the dominant mechanism driving an El Niño-like response is radiative cooling over tropical northern Africa (which weakens the West African Monsoon and the equatorial Atlantic trade winds), while the ocean dynamical thermostat mechanism is absent when uniform equatorial Pacific forcing is applied. Liu et al. [@liu2024] provide a reconstruction perspective, showing a weak El Niño-like response one year after large volcanic eruptions in an 800-year multiproxy δ¹⁸O reconstruction, with ENSO amplitude significantly correlated with volcanic intensity; Freund et al. [@freund2025enso] further situate this within the broader CESM LME framework, noting that the large number of last-millennium realisations is essential to disentangle the ENSO response from internal variability and eruption-specific preconditioning states. Together, these papers underscore that the iCESM LME's limited four-member ensemble (two eruptions) renders preconditioning-stratified conclusions inherently uncertain and motivates expansion to additional historical events.
:::

Recent work has further decomposed the pathways through which volcanic aerosols produce zonally asymmetric climate responses relevant to ENSO triggering. Andreasen et al. [@andreasen2025independent] demonstrate, using a state-of-the-art climate model, that the shortwave and longwave properties of stratospheric sulfate aerosols act through independent and fundamentally different mechanisms: shortwave cooling induces a tropospheric Rossby wave train connected to changes in tropical convection (consistent with the El Niño-like anomaly pathway documented by Pausata et al. [@pausata2023revisiting]), while longwave warming of the stratosphere alters stratospheric winds and produces a wavenumber-1 imprint on Northern Hemisphere surface temperatures. This independence implies that the net ENSO response depends on the relative magnitudes of these two competing forcings in any given eruption, adding uncertainty to deterministic triggering hypotheses. In the Indo-Pacific basin context, Tiger and Ummenhofer [@tiger2023tropical] find using the CESM-LME that strong tropical eruptions drive a robust negative Indian Ocean Dipole (IOD) in the eruption year followed by a positive IOD the following year, with the IOD and ENSO subsequently exhibiting a damped oscillatory response for up to eight years before returning to pre-eruption baselines; moreover, the initial IOD response is modulated by the pre-eruption phase of the Interdecadal Pacific Oscillation (IPO), consistent with the broader preconditioning picture established by Dogar et al. [@dogar2024nao] and Predybaylo et al. These findings expand the relevant state-space for interpreting El Chichón (1982) and Pinatubo (1991) in the iCESM LME by highlighting that the IOD background state at eruption time is an additional source of response variability beyond the Pacific ENSO preconditioning already examined.
:::

The reliability of iCESM pseudo-coral output as a proxy for observed δ¹⁸O_coral depends critically on how well the model reproduces the mean state and variability of seawater δ¹⁸O (δ¹⁸O_sw). Thompson et al. [@thompson2022hydrosensitive] demonstrate using both iCESM and the isotope-enabled Regional Ocean Modeling System (isoROMS) that δ¹⁸O_sw contributes up to 89% of δ¹⁸O_coral variability in the Western Pacific Warm Pool, yet find that iCESM substantially underestimates δ¹⁸O_sw variability and shows negative fractional covariance (F_cov) values there — a bias absent in the higher-resolution isoROMS — implying that iCESM pseudo-corals in the WPWP may under-represent the hydrological signal relative to the temperature signal. Complementing this, Stevenson et al. [@stevenson2023palmyra] present in-situ observations at Palmyra Atoll across two contrasting El Niño events (2014/15 and 2015/16) and show that advective ocean dynamics strongly modulate the seawater δ¹⁸O at coral sites in a highly non-stationary way; seawater δ¹⁸O changes can dominate the coral signal even in an event commonly characterised as "SST-driven," complicating event-by-event attribution when comparing iCESM pseudo-corals to iso2k observations. These findings motivate the bias-check framework developed for this chapter, which compares iCESM-simulated δ¹⁸O_sw against the LeGrande & Schmidt (2006) global climatology at all 89 iso2k coral sites before interpreting model–proxy discrepancies in the post-El Chichón and post-Pinatubo superposed-epoch analysis.
:::

The addition of reference SEA composites from seminal pre-2020 studies to the Ch2 figures folder today motivates a brief synthesis of how recent literature contextualises those classic benchmarks. The ocean dynamical thermostat (ODT) hypothesis — whereby symmetric radiative cooling preferentially warms the eastern tropical Pacific relative to the west, weakening trade winds and triggering El Niño via Bjerknes feedback — was the dominant mechanistic explanation in early analytic-model work (Clement et al. 1996; Emile-Geay et al. 2008). However, Pausata et al. [@pausata2023revisiting] show in a 40-member Earth System Model ensemble that the ODT mechanism is effectively absent when realistic volcanic aerosol is applied over the equatorial Pacific: instead, it is the asymmetric cooling of tropical northern Africa (NAFR) that drives equatorial westerly anomalies and an El Niño-like state, with land-surface heating contrasts communicating the eruption signal to the Pacific through atmospheric Kelvin waves. Liu et al. [@liu2022land] reach a complementary conclusion using targeted CESM sensitivity experiments — land aerosol forcing, particularly over sub-Saharan Africa, produces a faster and stronger El Niño response than ocean aerosol forcing alone, which instead excites a slower Indian Ocean dipole pathway. At the same time, Dogar et al. [@dogar2024nao] demonstrate using ENSO-preconditioned volcanic runs that the magnitude of the Niño 3.4 response is gated by the pre-eruption Pacific state: only simulations initialised in an El Niño-like state produce a robust post-eruption warming, while neutral and La Niña initial conditions yield only the direct volcanic cooling. Taken together, this body of work suggests that comparing the iCESM LME ensemble-mean SEA composite against classic multi-proxy benchmarks — which pool eruptions across all pre-eruption states — should account for ENSO preconditioning heterogeneity and for the now-questioned dominance of the ODT pathway in more complex coupled models.
:::

Today's refactoring of the Ch2 benchmark comparison scripts — specifically the correction of the El Chichón SO₂ attribution to Bluth et al. (1993) and the iso2k database attribution to Konecky et al. (2020) — motivates a brief note on the current state of volcanic aerosol forcing characterisation and its downstream implications for the iCESM pseudo-coral analysis. The stratospheric aerosol optical depth (SAOD)–effective radiative forcing (ERF) relationship used to prescribe volcanic forcing in last-millennium simulations is not a fixed linear scalar: Marshall et al. [@marshall2020forcing] demonstrate using a large ensemble of aerosol-chemistry-climate experiments that the ERF is on average ~20% less than the instantaneous radiative forcing due to a positive shortwave cloud adjustment, and that the SAOD–ERF conversion varies substantially with eruption latitude, season, and time since eruption. This implies that the relatively modest SO₂ loading of El Chichón relative to Pinatubo — the distinction originally flagged by Bluth et al. (1993) — may translate into a non-proportional difference in effective cooling, potentially explaining part of the asymmetry between the two eruptions in the iCESM LME SEA composites. Independently, Qin et al. [@qin2026pdv] construct a δ¹⁸O-based Pacific Decadal Variability (PDV) index using the same iCESM LME all-forcing and volcanic-only runs that underpin Ch2, and find that periods of intensive volcanic activity are associated with a shift from multi-decadal (50–70 yr) to bi-decadal (20–40 yr) preferred PDV timescales. This result suggests that the iCESM ensemble's mean-state response to El Chichón and Pinatubo embedded in the pseudo-coral SEA composites may be modulated by the background PDV phase in ways that single-eruption superposed-epoch analyses do not fully isolate. Finally, Liang et al. [@liang2025iod] ⚠ relevance uncertain offer an iso2k-informed data assimilation reconstruction showing that volcanic cooling in eruption years tends to precondition positive Indian Ocean Dipole events in the subsequent year — a teleconnection pathway that could influence the Western Pacific δ¹⁸O signal that Ch2's pseudo-corals track.
