## Themes

### Volcanic forcing of ENSO _(added 2026-04-17)_

Scholar Gateway · "How do volcanic eruptions modulate ENSO variability and tropical Pacific sea surface temperatures?" · 8 passages · 5 articles · 2022-12-12–2024-05-16.

The dominant mechanistic view holds that large tropical eruptions can trigger an El Niño–like response through an "ocean dynamical thermostat": aerosol-induced surface cooling produces a smaller SST drop in the eastern Pacific than the western Pacific because eastern upwelling advects deeper, already-cool water, weakening the zonal SST gradient and trade winds via the Bjerknes feedback (Liu et al., 2022). This framing is complicated by evidence that the response is *preconditioned* on initial ocean state: Dogar et al. (2024) find that positive-ENSO–preconditioned volcanic runs produce El Niño–like anomalies up to 2 °C after eruption, while neutral and La Niña initial states yield no ENSO-like modulation, implying that "volcano → El Niño" is not a deterministic rule. Proxy reconstructions tell a consistent but muted story: Liu et al. (2024) reconstruct ENSO over the last millennium from a global δ18O network and recover only a weak El Niño response one year after major eruptions, though ENSO amplitude is significantly correlated with cumulative volcanic intensity (r ≈ 0.46 in CESM-LME). A separate coral-based reconstruction from the South China Sea (665–749 CE) attributes an abrupt 39% increase in ENSO variability at ~700 CE to combined solar and volcanic forcing, demonstrating that external forcing competes with internal climate dynamics to shape tropical Pacific variability (Jiang et al., 2023). Finally, Liu et al. (2022) isolate the land-vs-ocean partition of volcanic aerosol forcing and show that aerosol over tropical African land — via monsoon suppression and Kelvin-wave westerly anomalies — is *more* effective at triggering an El Niño than aerosol confined to ocean, a mechanistic detail that most "volcano-ENSO" bulk studies obscure.

## Gaps & open questions

- Preconditioning vs. deterministic response: the literature is actively unresolved on whether tropical eruptions *cause* El Niño or merely *amplify* ongoing warm states (Dogar et al., 2024; Liu et al., 2024). Chapter analysis should stratify eruption cases by initial ENSO phase rather than averaging across events.
- Pseudo-coral δ18O signal-to-noise under volcanic forcing has not been systematically characterized across the δ18O proxy network used in Liu et al. (2024) — a sensitivity-analysis opportunity.
- Land-vs-ocean aerosol partitioning (Liu et al., 2022) is absent from most last-millennium ensemble runs, which apply zonally uniform forcing by default.

The dependence of the post-volcanic ENSO response on ocean preconditioning is increasingly recognised as a first-order modulator of response amplitude and timing. Dogar et al. [@dogar2024nao] demonstrate using GFDL-CM2.1 that El Niño-preconditioned eruptions produce El Niño-like anomalies reaching up to +2 °C in Niño 3.4, whereas neutral and La Niña-preconditioned simulations show no Pacific warming signal beyond the aerosol-induced cooling — a finding that challenges the earlier expectation, rooted in the ocean dynamical thermostat mechanism [@clement1996ocean], that La Niña preconditioning would amplify the post-eruption El Niño response via thermocline reservoir release. Complementing this, Pausata et al. [@pausata2023revisiting] use sensitivity experiments with regionally partitioned aerosol loading to show that the dominant mechanism driving an El Niño-like response is radiative cooling over tropical northern Africa (which weakens the West African Monsoon and the equatorial Atlantic trade winds), while the ocean dynamical thermostat mechanism is absent when uniform equatorial Pacific forcing is applied. Liu et al. [@liu2024] provide a reconstruction perspective, showing a weak El Niño-like response one year after large volcanic eruptions in an 800-year multiproxy δ¹⁸O reconstruction, with ENSO amplitude significantly correlated with volcanic intensity; Freund et al. [@freund2025enso] further situate this within the broader CESM LME framework, noting that the large number of last-millennium realisations is essential to disentangle the ENSO response from internal variability and eruption-specific preconditioning states. Together, these papers underscore that the iCESM LME's limited four-member ensemble (two eruptions) renders preconditioning-stratified conclusions inherently uncertain and motivates expansion to additional historical events.
